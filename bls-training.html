Asphyxia Protocol

Choking: What To Do in the First 60 Seconds

Mechanical airway obstruction requires structural physical clearance. Zero delay permitted.

⏱️ 60s Window

How to know it's serious (Complete Airway Obstruction):

  • Your child is completely unable to cry, speak, or cough out loud.
  • Inability to clear airway autonomously; silent gasping or frantic gestures.
  • Cyanosis setting in: lips, tongue, or nailbeds starting to turn dusky or bluish.
▶
🎥 Watch Demonstration: Pediatric Heimlich & Infant Back Blows

If your child is UNDER 1 year (Infant)

1

Positioning: Hold your baby face-down along your forearm, resting on your thigh, with the head supported lower than the body.

2

Back Blows: Deliver 5 firm back blows forcefully between the shoulder blades using the heel of your hand.

3

Chest Thrusts: If object is not freed, turn the baby face-up along your opposite arm. Provide 5 chest thrusts on the center of the chest using two fingers.

4

Repeat Cycle: Continuously alternate 5 blows and 5 thrusts until the object is expelled or the infant goes limp.

If your child is OVER 1 year (Toddler/Child)

1

Positioning: Stand or kneel directly behind your child and wrap your arms completely around their waist.

2

Hand Placement: Make a fist with one hand and place the thumb side just slightly above their belly button (well away from the breastbone).

3

Abdominal Thrusts: Grasp your fist with your other hand and give quick, distinct, inward and upward thrusts into the abdomen.

4

Execution: Administer thrusts sequentially until the foreign body is successfully dislodged.

❌ CRITICAL MISTAKES TO AVOID

  • No Blind Finger Sweeps: Never insert your fingers blindly into the throat. You risk forcing the object deeper into the narrow larynx.
  • No Liquids: Do not give water or try to "flush it down"—this fills up what little airway remains.
  • No Delayed Calls: Call for emergency help immediately if the child becomes unconscious or breathing stops.

Epistaxis Management

Nosebleed: What To Do in the First 10 Minutes

Prevent blood aspiration and encourage immediate localized coagulation.

⏱️ 10 Min Control

How to assess stability:

Blood coming from one or both nostrils is terrifying but manageable if the child is fully alert, sitting up, and responding normally to voice commands.

Step-by-Step Mechanical Control

1

Sit Upright & Tilt Forward: Have your child sit upright. Keep their head tilted slightly forward. This forces blood out of the nostrils instead of draining down the throat.

2

Pinch the Soft Tissue: Using your thumb and index finger, firmly pinch the soft, fleshy lower portion of the nose just below the bony bridge.

3

Maintain Steady Compression: Hold compression continuously for 10 full minutes by the clock. Do not let go early to "check if it's working"—releasing pressure breaks the early clotting mesh.

4

Mouth Breathing: Reassure the child and direct them to breathe calmly through their mouth while pressure is applied.

❌ CRITICAL MISTAKES TO AVOID

  • Never Tilt the Head Backward: Tilting backward causes blood to run into the stomach and throat, triggering gagging, coughing, or vomiting.
  • No Deep Stuffing: Do not pack tissues, gauze, or cotton balls deep inside the nasal cavities. Removing them later peels away the freshly formed scab.

✅ WHEN TO SEEK MEDICAL HELP

  • The bleeding does not stop after 20 continuous minutes of direct manual pressure.
  • The nosebleed started directly after a hard fall, sports collision, or physical head injury.
  • The child feels lightheaded, unusually dizzy, weak, or completely pale.
Anaphylaxis Prevention

Allergic Reactions: Systemic Escalation Protocol

Recognize signs of airway compromise before severe distress sets in.

⚠️ Rapid Escalation

How to identify a severe systemic reaction (Anaphylaxis):

  • Sudden swelling spreading across the face, lips, tongue, or eyelid margins.
  • Respiratory signs: persistent dry cough, high-pitched wheezing, or visible chest retractions.
  • Gastrointestinal/Neurological: unexpected vomiting or profound lethargy and drowsiness.

Immediate Care Sequence

1

Check Airway: Assess breathing depth immediately. If you hear any wheezing or structural struggling, proceed to immediate intervention.

2

Administer Epinephrine: If the child has a known allergy and an auto-injector is prescribed, inject it into the outer thigh muscle without delay.

3

Call Emergency Medical Teams: Always secure professional evaluation after epinephrine delivery, as a secondary reaction wave can occur hours later.

4

Optimal Positioning: Keep the child lying flat on their back with legs slightly raised to maintain optimal blood pressure, unless breathing is too difficult.

❌ CRITICAL MISTAKES TO AVOID

  • Do Not Wait to See: Never monitor a child "for an hour" to see if a full-body rash improves on its own if they are wheezing.
  • Do Not Rely on Antihistamines Alone: Simple oral syrups cannot reverse sudden airway swelling during anaphylaxis; only epinephrine can.
Thermal Injury Care

Burns & Scalds: Thermal Energy Reduction

Arrest progressive deep-tissue cooking by dissipating residual thermal heat.

⏱️ 20 Min Cooling
▶
🎥 Watch Demonstration: Managing Burns & Proper Dressing Techniques

The First 20 Minutes Matter Most

1

Remove Heat Source: Safely detach the child from the source of heat (boiling liquids, hot iron, electrical appliances).

2

Cool Under Running Water: Place the affected skin under cool, gently running tap water for a full 10 to 20 minutes. This stops the burn from eating deeper into the underlying layers.

3

Clear Obstructions Carefully: Loosen or cut away clothing near the burn, but never pull off material that has melted and stuck directly to the raw wound.

4

Apply Non-Adherent Dressing: Cover the cooled area lightly with clean plastic wrap or a sterile non-stick bandage to block airborne bacteria.

❌ NEVER APPLY HOME REMEDIES

  • No Ice: Extreme ice application constricts blood vessels, deepening the tissue damage.
  • No Toothpaste, Oils, or Butter: These compounds trap the heat inside the skin and introduce harmful microbes directly into the broken tissue layer.
  • Never Pop Blisters: Intact blisters form a natural sterile seal to protect the raw dermis below. Breaking them causes infection.
Toxicology First Response

Poisoning & Accidental Ingestion: Chemical Control

Prevent corrosive re-exposure of the esophagus and upper respiratory tract.

🚨 Call Emergency

Signs of toxic ingestion:

Unusual residue or severe chemical chemical burns around the mouth or lips, strange chemical breath, sudden unexplained drowsiness, vomiting, or open pill bottles nearby.

Immediate Countermeasures

1

Clear Immediate Danger: Calmly take away any remaining toxic pills or chemical bottles from the child’s grasp.

2

Preserve the Compound Label: Save the exact container, pill box, or chemical label. Medical staff need this data to identify the specific toxin and its antidote.

3

Prevent Emesis: Keep the child seated upright. Monitor closely for any sudden neurological changes or drops in responsiveness.

4

Urgent Transport: Rush to the nearest emergency unit immediately. Do not wait for visible toxic signs or sickness to show up.

❌ DO NOT INDUCE VOMITING

Never stick your fingers down the throat or force home remedies to induce vomiting. If the child swallowed a corrosive chemical (like toilet cleaner or bleach), forcing it back up burns the esophagus a second time, and can easily choke their lungs.

Envenomation Control

Bee Sting: Localized vs Systemic Action

Neutralize venom entry and minimize the physical spread of the toxin localized to the bite site.

🩹 Local Care

Step-by-Step Immediate Management

1

Scrape the Stinger Out: Remove the stinger instantly by scraping across it using a fingernail or the flat edge of a credit card. Do not pull it with fingers.

2

Sanitize the Area: Wash the sting thoroughly with plain soap and cool water to lower the chance of secondary skin infection.

3

Cold Compress: Apply a clean ice pack wrapped in a cloth for 10 minutes to reduce regional throbbing and swelling.

What To Monitor Separately

Mild Reaction: Expect pinpoint pain, red skin, and minor puffiness localized entirely around the sting site.

Systemic Watch: Keep looking for swelling traveling up the limb, facial puffiness, hives elsewhere, or wheezing. If these appear, jump directly to the Allergic Reaction Protocol.

❌ DO NOT SQUEEZE THE STINGER

Never squeeze the stinger with tweezers or your fingertips. Squeezing compresses the attached venom sac, pumping the remaining poison directly into your child’s skin.

Bronchospasm Relief

Asthma Attack: Emergency Inhaler Flow

Optimize immediate air delivery to narrow, spasming lung passages during acute flare-ups.

🫁 Open Airways

How to identify an acute asthma crisis:

The child is breathing rapidly, wheezing loudly when breathing out, shows visible neck and rib strain while drawing breath, or is struggling to finish a sentence without stopping for air.

Immediate Rescue Steps

1

Sit Completely Upright: Sit the child straight up instantly. Do not let them lean back or lie down, which constricts lung expansion.

2

Use Spacer with Inhaler: Connect their prescribed blue reliever inhaler (Salbutamol) to a spacer. Deliver 1 puff at a time, letting the child take 4 slow breaths for each puff. Repeat as directed by your doctor.

3

Calm Reassurance: Speak in a steady voice. Panic increases muscle tension, which worsens breathing difficulty.

⚠️ CRITICAL WARNING FOR PARENTS

If the child shows no real improvement 5 minutes after using their rescue inhaler, struggles to talk, or turns pale or blue around the lips, call for an emergency vehicle immediately. Keep giving doses through the spacer while you wait.

Seizures: Protecting the Airway

Manage physical safety during involuntary electrical storms in the brain.

🧠 Protect, Don't Control

Recognizing Seizure Variants:

Look for full-body stiffness, rhythmic jerking of limbs, rolled-back eyes, heavy drooling, or a sudden blank stare with a total loss of awareness.

▶
🎥 Watch Demonstration: Dr. Namrata on Proper Lateral Seizure Positioning

Safety Steps During a Seizure

1

Gently Roll Onto the Side: Guide your child onto their side (recovery position) as soon as possible. This keeps their airway open and lets saliva or vomit drain safely out of the mouth.

2

Clear Physical Obstacles: Quickly clear away hard toys, sharp furniture, or nearby objects to prevent injury from involuntary flailing.

3

Cushion the Head: Place a soft folded jacket, sweater, or small pillow gently beneath their head to shield it from hard floor impacts.

4

Time the Fit: Note the exact start and end time of the jerking. This timeline is critical data for the pediatric team.

❌ CRITICAL SEIZURE MYTHS TO AVOID

  • Never Restrain Movements: Do not try to force down or pin a child’s limbs to stop the shaking. This can cause bone fractures or muscle tears.
  • Do Not Put Items in the Mouth: Never place wooden spoons, metal keys, or fingers into their mouth. Children cannot "swallow their tongue," but inserting objects can break teeth or block their breathing.
  • Call 1066 Instantly If: The continuous shaking lasts longer than 5 minutes, if it is the child’s first seizure, or if they take a long time to wake up afterward.
Neurological System Check

High Fever with Unresponsiveness: Emergency Protocol

Differentiate simple viral heat lethargy from true clinical central nervous system depression.

🌡️ Urgent Care

Immediate Assessment & Action

1

Verify Responsiveness: Call your child's name loudly and rub your knuckles firmly over their chest bone. See if they open their eyes or respond to the physical touch.

2

Begin Lukewarm Sponging: If they are breathing but hard to wake, strip off heavy clothes and wipe their skin with a washcloth dipped in lukewarm tap water. Never use freezing water.

3

Maintain Lateral Open Airway: If they are sleepy but breathing, lay them on their side to keep their airway safe from saliva while you arrange medical transport.

What To Look Out For

High Alert: Watch for a stiff neck, extreme sensitivity to room light, unexpected vomiting, or small, dark red or purple spots on the skin that don't fade when pressed firmly with a clear glass.

❌ DO NOT CHOKE AN UNCONSCIOUS CHILD

Never pour paracetamol syrup, water, or home remedies into the mouth of a child who is drowsy or unresponsive. If they cannot swallow properly, the liquid will go straight into their lungs, causing aspiration pneumonia.

Venom Immobilization Flow

Snake Bite: Preventing Systematic Envenomation

Keep the venom localized at the bite site to slow down its spread through the body's lymphatic system.

🐍 Venom Stop

Identifying features:

Look for double puncture marks, sudden localized swelling, changing skin color, sharp pain, or unexpected drowsiness after outdoor play.

Immediate Field Management Steps

1

Absolute Physical Immobility: Keep your child completely still and quiet. Running or crying speeds up their heart rate, pumping the snake venom through their body much faster.

2

Positioning: Lay them down flat. Keep the bitten arm or leg resting below the level of their heart.

3

Immobilize the Limb: Wrap a clean, broad bandage snugly over the limb and tie on a straight wooden splint or rolled newspapers to keep the joint from bending.

4

Remove Tight Items: Take off tight shoes, socks, rings, or bracelets immediately. Snakebite swelling spreads fast, and tight items can cut off circulation completely.

❌ DEADLY OUTDATED MYTHS (NEVER ATTEMPT)

  • Do NOT Cut the Wound: Cutting the skin open deepens infection risks and does not remove the venom.
  • Do NOT Suck Out Venom: Never try to suck the venom out with your mouth. It doesn't work and introduces venom straight into the adult's bloodstream through small mouth sores.
  • No Tourniquets or Ice: Tying tight ropes cuts off blood flow completely, which can destroy the limb and lead to amputation. Ice damages the bitten skin even further.
Household Preparedness

Basic First Aid Checklist for Every Household

Keep these essential supplies packed and ready in an accessible box out of your children's reach.

🏠 Home Safety

Essential First Aid Box Supplies

✓

Sterile gauze pads of assorted sizes & hypoallergenic medical tape rolls.

✓

Antiseptic skin solution (chlorhexidine or betadine) for minor scrapes.

✓

Digital thermometer along with updated pediatric dosing charts for fever syrup.

✓

Elastic compression bandages for ankle or wrist sprains.

Crucial Household Safety Actions

1

Store all household cleaning liquids and medical prescriptions locked up high away from curious hands.

2

Keep emergency helpline contacts printed clearly on your refrigerator door.

3

Have grandparents and domestic helpers take basic child safety training workshops.
